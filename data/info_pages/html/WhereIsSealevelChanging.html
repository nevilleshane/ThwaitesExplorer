Summary

Sea-level is affected by many factors and does not change the same way everywhere on Earth. Locally-defined sea-level, known as relative sea-level, has the largest impact upon our communities. Averaged across the globe, sea-level has risen about 200mm over the past 100 years, but locally in some coastal regions, sea-levels can be far from the average, rising faster or in some areas falling. Local vertical land movement plays a big part in determining the height of relative sea-level and can result from natural processes such the land rebounding following earlier ice covering, or earthquake-induced motion.

What causes sea level to change? Sea level changes through time as a result of a range of Earth processes that can be explored through this app. In the past century, the absolute height of average sea-level has risen globally by approximately 200 mm. Over that time about half of that rise has been due to thermal expansion of the warming ocean water, and the majority of the remainder has come from additional discharged into the ocean by melting land glaciers, and in a smaller amount melting from the ice sheets. The Permanent Service for Mean Sea Level (PSMSL) was established in 1993 to collect, publish, analyze and interpret sea level data from a permanent global network of tide stations. Based in Liverpool at the National Oceanography Centre (NOC) it relies on member nations to contribute their monthly data. The image shows the GLOSS Core Network of 300 tide stations around the world allocated for baseline data. These sites are located on islands to maximize exposure to the open ocean. Most studies of 20th century global sea level rise are based on the PSMSL data set.

The The global network of backbone tide gauges used to determine global sea level rise amounts. (Image credit:PSML)

What is happening with sea level globally? For most of the past century, the change in global sea-level occurred at a pace of about 1.7 mm/year. However, tide gauge data and satellite measurements show that since the early 1990s sea-level rise has been accelerating, rising at about 3.2 mm/year, twice as fast as before. These are average values. In some locations, the rise in sea-level has been greater than the average rate, while in other areas there has actually been a drop in average sea-level over the same time frame. This map from NOAA is of tide gauge measurements showing relative sea-level in each location. On the map view (and in the image to the below) you see areas of the Gulf Coast and sections of the Northeastern U.S. that are experiencing a pronounced rise in sea-level. In coastal Louisiana, pronounced land subsidence leads to relative sea-level rising at about 10 mm/year. In contrast, in part of Alaska, the strong vertical land uplift due to tectonic motion combines with post-glacial rebound to result in a fall in relative sea-level of as much as 15 mm/year. These differences can be explained by vertical changes in the shoreline from either subsidence or uplift as explained below.

Relative sea-level is rising rapidly along the Gulf Coast shoreline and the Chesapeake Bay area and is dropping in parts of Alaska. (Image credit:EPA-NOAA)

What is relative sea-level? Relative sea-level refers to the mean height of the ocean at one particular location. There are different factors affecting relative sea-level that include ocean density, atmospheric pressure, seasonal weather patterns, the movement of sediments, and anthropogenic influences such as dredging and the draining of wetlands. However, the major players are the interaction between the warming and thermally-expanding ocean and up and down vertical movements of the land surface. In places where the land is subsiding or moving downwards at the same time that absolute sea-level is rising, the local, relative rise in sea-level can be dramatic. Land subsidence is causing faster-than-average rates of sea-level rise around many parts of the eastern and southern shorelines in the U.S. Some of the subsidence is driven by natural earth processes, but some is aggravated by human activities.

How can humans affect sea-level? The Galveston-Houston area of Texas and Virginia's Chesapeake Bay region have both been affected by the decades-long removal of freshwater from underground aquifers, with the Texas locality also impacted by oil and gas extraction. The map below, for Chesapeake Bay, shows that groundwater levels in the aquifer have dropped by as much as 65 m in the 20th Century. The corresponding reduction in groundwater pressure causes the land surface to subside as the sedimentary layers compress.

In these parts of Texas and Virginia, the sinking land and rising ocean water produce a local sea-level rise of about 6 mm/year (Texas) and 4 mm/year (Virginia). But the story in the Chesapeake Bay is complex with natural and human induced contributions involved. 35 million years ago a large meteor impacted the bay by Norfolk VA. weakening the sediment in a wide 80 km stretch. This weakening may still be contributing to the land subsidence. In addition, this area is still undergoing glacial isostatic adjustment after the last Glacial Maximum. This area sat on the edge of the land that was popped up like the end of a lever by the weight of the ice sheet further north. In coastal Louisiana, sea-level is rising even faster, at a rate of 9-11 mm/year which is the fastest rate in the U.S. and one of the fastest rates in the world. That high rate results from the combination of global sea-level rise and local, rapid land subsidence due to compaction of Mississippi delta sediment and large-scale engineering projects that reduce the flow of replenishing sediments that once flooded the area regularly. We saw one effect of this subsidence when in 2005, Hurricane Katrina hit the Gulf coast and the city of New Orleans. Storm surge measuring 3-6 m flooded areas further inland, causing destruction to the highly settled region.

What else causes sea-level to fall? There are some areas of the world where relative sea-level is actually falling because the land is moving upwards at a rate faster than absolute global sea-level is rising. There are several causes for this. During the last ice age, northern hemisphere ice sheets were centered upon Hudson Bay in northeastern Canada (the Laurentide ice sheet) and upon Fennoscandia (the Eurasian ice sheet). Their immense weight pushed down the land beneath, is hard for us to imagine. You can use the interactive that is linked in the image to the left that lets you click through time, starting 120,000 years ago in a relatively warm period, through to the most recent ice age and the growth of the Laurentide ice sheet, and then up to present. See how the land deforms under the weight of the ice, and the linked impacts on global sea level. Additionally, the image below shows how the ice weight pushes down on the land directly below, while the edges (called the peripheral bulge) are popped up. It is this combination of processes that directly affects sea level. Together geologists refer to this process as glacial isostatic adjustment and the timing of the land response is slow with uplift occurring over thousands of years.

The map of Scandinavia below shows that the rate of uplift is as much as 10 mm/year. Similar rates are measured for Hudson Bay. Areas once popped up on the glacial peripheral bulge are now subsiding as the mantle beneath returns to a state of equilibrium. As mentioned in the discussion above on the Chesapeake Bay region, elevations across much of the northeastern U.S. below New York, some Baltic nations and the south of England are subsiding because of this glacial rebound effect. This contributes to the rise in relative sea-level, in some cases of up to a few millimeters per year.

Regionally, the vertical movement of land can be complicated to understand. Parts of southern Alaska that were once depressed beneath the weight of regional glaciers and smaller ice sheets are rapidly being uplifted as you can see on the map. At the same time, the complex interaction of the Pacific and North American tectonic plates and the smaller Yakutat terrane, a small fragment of crustal material that is attaching itself to the Alaskan coast, is causing additional rapid uplift of the land. Here the combined effect of the post-glacial rebound and tectonic forces greatly exceeds the rise in absolute sea-level, resulting in the localised relative sea-level dropping very quickly. At rates of 10-15 mm/year, these are some of the fastest retreats of sea-level on Earth. Plate tectonics is also responsible for more drastic changes in relative sea-level. For example, the catastrophic magnitude 9 Tohoku earthquake of March 2011 caused a significant area of land in eastern Japan to instantaneously drop below sea-level.

The dynamics of the ocean and atmosphere also play a role. In the Western Pacific Ocean, sea-level has been rising at three times the global average since 1993, perhaps due to an intensification of the trade winds that piles up ocean water there. In another example, the mid-Atlantic Drift could produce a rise in sea-level of an extra 200 mm in New York City. In that scenario, warming of the north Atlantic and increased melting of Greenland glaciers could alter the temperature and density of the seawater enough to reduce the strength of the Gulf Stream which, at present, sweeps ocean water away from the U.S. northeast coastline and into the strong flow. If that flow is reduced, the water could move closer to shore. Additionally, some research suggests that the gravitational pull exerted by the massive Antarctic and Greenland ice sheets attracts ocean water and effectively lowers sea-level in other parts of the global ocean. For example, if the West Antarctic Ice Sheet was to melt, which would take considerable time, the gravitational attraction around West Antarctica itself would weaken and the water that had been attracted there would move away resulting in additional sea-level rise in the northern hemisphere. This change could add as much as a meter or two on top of the expected ~6 m of sea level rise from the melted ice sheet itself.
